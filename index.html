Introduction

The Akash Missile represents India’s ambition to achieve self-reliance in strategic defense systems. Designed as a shield against aerial threats, it plays a vital role in protecting high-value military assets and infrastructure from enemy aircraft, missiles, and unmanned aerial systems.

Akash was envisioned to close the gap between short-range tactical defenses and long-range strategic deterrence. By empowering the armed forces with mobile, multi-target air defense capability, it strengthens India’s layered missile defense doctrine and provides coverage for both static bases and dynamic formations.

What sets Akash apart is not just its missile, but the complete system — integrating surveillance, tracking, fire control, and engagement in a seamless loop. Built entirely with indigenous technologies, Akash reflects India's growing technological maturity in the defense sector.

Since its inception, the system has continually evolved to address emerging aerial threats, from legacy aircraft to next-generation stealth platforms and high-speed UAVs. As a result, Akash has become more than a missile — it's a platform for innovation, modernization, and strategic autonomy.

Development Timeline

1983 – IGMDP Launched

The Indian government launches the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme to develop five indigenous missiles — Prithvi, Agni, Trishul, Nag, and Akash.

1990 – First Test Flight

Akash conducts its first successful test flight, initiating flight validation and design development.

2005–2006 – Interception Trials

Akash demonstrates its ability to intercept multiple high-speed aerial targets simultaneously, validating radar coordination and missile agility.

Dec 2007 – IAF User Trials

The Indian Air Force successfully completes 10 days of user trials at Chandipur, with all five interceptions hitting their targets.

2010 – Akash Mk-I Induction (IAF)

Akash Mk-I is inducted into the Indian Air Force for operational air defense. The initial version provides medium-range surface-to-air defense capability.

May 2015 – Army Deployment

The Indian Army begins deploying Akash Mk-I for mobile battlefield air defense, mounted on wheeled and tracked platforms.

May 2019 – Akash-1S Tested

Akash-1S is successfully tested with an indigenous seeker, enhancing its ability to intercept smaller, faster threats such as cruise missiles and UAVs.

Jan 2021 – Akash-NG Maiden Test

The next-generation Akash-NG is test-fired with a canister launch system and an active radar seeker. It features longer range, lighter design, and improved agility.

July 2021 – NG Follow-up Tests

Akash-NG proves its effectiveness by intercepting high-speed aerial targets during follow-up validation trials.

Jan 2024 – NG UAV Intercept

Akash-NG successfully intercepts a low-altitude, high-speed UAV, confirming its readiness for operational deployment in real-world scenarios.

Akash Missile Variants

The Akash missile family has evolved through multiple upgrades, each version improving upon range, precision, and operational flexibility to address modern aerial threats.

  • Akash Mk-I: The original version featuring a command-guided interception system and a range of up to 30 km. Inducted into the Indian Air Force and Army.
  • Akash-1S: An improved version equipped with an indigenous seeker, enhancing accuracy against small and agile aerial targets like cruise missiles and drones.
  • Akash Prime: Incorporates a refined RF seeker and improved performance in extreme weather and altitude, making it effective against low-altitude threats.
  • Akash-NG: The next-generation system with a longer range (up to 80 km), AESA radar, canister launch, and multiple target engagement capabilities.

Key Features

  • Range: Up to 30 km
  • Altitude: Up to 18 km
  • Speed: Mach 2.5–3.0
  • Warhead: 60 kg HE fragmentation
  • Guidance: Ground command via Rajendra Radar
  • Mobility: Mounted on wheeled & tracked vehicles
  • Variants: Akash, Akash-1S, Akash-NG

How It Works

Akash uses command guidance, where radar tracks both target and missile. Ground systems send real-time corrections to guide the missile. A proximity fuse detonates near the target, maximizing hit probability.

Missile Trajectory

The Akash missile follows a semi-ballistic path. It ascends post-launch, then uses continuous radar updates to adjust its flight mid-air until impact.
