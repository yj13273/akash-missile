Introduction

The Akash Missile represents India’s ambition to achieve self-reliance in strategic defense systems. Designed as a shield against aerial threats, it plays a vital role in protecting high-value military assets and infrastructure from enemy aircraft, missiles, and unmanned aerial systems.

Akash was envisioned to close the gap between short-range tactical defenses and long-range strategic deterrence. By empowering the armed forces with mobile, multi-target air defense capability, it strengthens India’s layered missile defense doctrine and provides coverage for both static bases and dynamic formations.

What sets Akash apart is not just its missile, but the complete system — integrating surveillance, tracking, fire control, and engagement in a seamless loop. Built entirely with indigenous technologies, Akash reflects India's growing technological maturity in the defense sector.

Since its inception, the system has continually evolved to address emerging aerial threats, from legacy aircraft to next-generation stealth platforms and high-speed UAVs. As a result, Akash has become more than a missile — it's a platform for innovation, modernization, and strategic autonomy.

Development Timeline

1983 – IGMDP Launched

The Indian government launches the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme to develop five indigenous missiles — Prithvi, Agni, Trishul, Nag, and Akash.

1990 – First Test Flight

Akash conducts its first successful test flight, initiating flight validation and design development.

2005–2006 – Interception Trials

Akash demonstrates its ability to intercept multiple high-speed aerial targets simultaneously, validating radar coordination and missile agility.

Dec 2007 – IAF User Trials

The Indian Air Force successfully completes 10 days of user trials at Chandipur, with all five interceptions hitting their targets.

2010 – Akash Mk-I Induction (IAF)

Akash Mk-I is inducted into the Indian Air Force for operational air defense. The initial version provides medium-range surface-to-air defense capability.

May 2015 – Army Deployment

The Indian Army begins deploying Akash Mk-I for mobile battlefield air defense, mounted on wheeled and tracked platforms.

May 2019 – Akash-1S Tested

Akash-1S is successfully tested with an indigenous seeker, enhancing its ability to intercept smaller, faster threats such as cruise missiles and UAVs.

Jan 2021 – Akash-NG Maiden Test

The next-generation Akash-NG is test-fired with a canister launch system and an active radar seeker. It features longer range, lighter design, and improved agility.

July 2021 – NG Follow-up Tests

Akash-NG proves its effectiveness by intercepting high-speed aerial targets during follow-up validation trials.

Jan 2024 – NG UAV Intercept

Akash-NG successfully intercepts a low-altitude, high-speed UAV, confirming its readiness for operational deployment in real-world scenarios.

Akash Missile Variants

  • Akash Mk-I: The original version featuring a command-guided interception system and a range of up to 30 km. Inducted into the Indian Air Force and Army.
  • Akash-1S: An improved version equipped with an indigenous seeker, enhancing accuracy against small and agile aerial targets like cruise missiles and drones.
  • Akash Prime: Incorporates a refined RF seeker and improved performance in extreme weather and altitude, making it effective against low-altitude threats.
  • Akash-NG: The next-generation system with a longer range (up to 80 km), AESA radar, canister launch, and multiple target engagement capabilities.

Key Features

  • Range: Up to 30 km
  • Altitude: Up to 18 km
  • Speed: Mach 2.5–3.0
  • Warhead: 60 kg HE fragmentation
  • Guidance: Ground command via Rajendra Radar
  • Mobility: Mounted on wheeled & tracked vehicles
  • Variants: Akash, Akash-1S, Akash-NG

Working of Akash-NG Missile System

The Akash-NG missile system begins its engagement process with target detection using the indigenously developed Active Electronically Scanned Array (AESA) Multifunction Radar (MFR). Unlike passive radars, AESA enables:

  • High refresh rate scanning for faster detection cycles
  • Simultaneous multi-target detection and tracking
  • Low Probability of Intercept (LPI) capability
  • X-band operation for high-resolution tracking of low-RCS targets
  • Its look-down/shoot-down capability enables tracking of low-flying aircraft despite ground clutter interference.

Upon identifying a potential threat (within 80–100 km range), the radar passes tracking information to the Fire Control System (FCS).

Once detection is confirmed, 3D tracking (range, azimuth, elevation) begins instantly. The Battle Management Software (BMS) processes:

  • Threat classification using parameters like altitude, velocity vector, and maneuver profile
  • Detection and analysis of electronic countermeasure (ECM) activity
  • Real-time threat prioritization through decision algorithms
  • Concurrent intercept planning for multiple high-priority targets

The Fire Control System (FCS), in coordination with the Weapon Control Computer (WCC), orchestrates the missile launch sequence. This process encompasses:

  • Real-time trajectory computation using radar tracking data and engagement geometry
  • Comprehensive pre-launch diagnostics and health status verification of the missile
  • Launcher alignment based on target azimuth and elevation, followed by system-level launch authorization

The entire sequence—from target detection to missile launch—is executed in under 15 seconds, ensuring rapid response to high-speed aerial threats.

The Akash-NG missile is vertically launched from a sealed, canisterized system designed for rapid deployment and survivability. This configuration offers:

  • Low visual and infrared signature, minimizing detectability during launch
  • All-terrain mobility, enabling launch from rugged or remote battlefield locations
  • Dual-pulse solid-propellant motor, delivering sustained thrust and maneuverability throughout flight
  • Acceleration to speeds exceeding Mach 2.5 (857.5 m/s) within seconds, ensuring swift interception of high-velocity targets

Mid-course navigation in Akash-NG integrates onboard autonomy with real-time ground-based updates:

  • An Inertial Navigation System (INS) provides self-contained flight path control
  • A secure command data link transmits continuous updates from the fire control unit
  • Trajectory corrections are applied dynamically to counter target maneuvers
  • Designed for network-centric warfare, it is highly resilient to electronic countermeasures (ECM)

In the final phase, the Akash-NG uses its Ka-band Active Radar Seeker to autonomously lock onto the target, enabling fire-and-forget capability. The seeker:

  • Tracks fast, low-RCS, or maneuvering targets with high accuracy
  • Operates effectively under jamming and electronic countermeasures (ECM)
  • Continuously refines trajectory for a precise intercept

This ensures high terminal accuracy and reliability, even in contested airspace.

On reaching 15–30 meters from the target:

  • The proximity fuse triggers the warhead, initiating detonation
  • It disperses thousands of high-density, pre-fragmented metal shards at hypersonic speeds
  • This results in area-effect kinetic fragmentation, capable of neutralizing high-speed or stealth targets
  • Even near misses are lethal due to the sheer velocity and spread of the fragments

Missile Trajectory of Akash-NG

The Akash-NG missile follows a sophisticated trajectory composed of several critical flight phases designed for maximum efficiency and interception probability.

🚀 1. Vertical Boost Phase

Upon launch from a canisterized system, the missile initiates a vertical ascent to quickly clear the launcher and surrounding terrain, reducing vulnerability to counterfire.

📈 2. Attitude Adjustment & Pitch-Over

Using onboard gyros and Inertial Navigation System (INS), the missile performs a controlled pitch-over maneuver toward the predicted intercept point.

🧭 3. Mid-Course Guidance

During this phase, ground-based radar data and onboard INS provide updated navigation commands via secure datalink, allowing real-time trajectory corrections.

🎯 4. Terminal Homing

The onboard Active Radar Seeker activates in the final 10–15 km, autonomously locking onto the target and adjusting the flight path for a direct hit using proportional navigation.

💥 5. Intercept & Detonation

At proximity (15–30 m), the proximity fuse activates, detonating the warhead and dispersing high-velocity fragments to neutralize even highly maneuverable or stealth targets.
